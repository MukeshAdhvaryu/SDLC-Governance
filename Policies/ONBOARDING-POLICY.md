# Onboarding Policy

## The Problem This Policy Exists to Solve

A new engineer who spends their first two weeks waiting for a laptop, a repository
invitation, and a VPN token is not being onboarded. They are being processed.

The difference matters. Processing makes them feel like a liability the organisation
is reluctantly absorbing. Onboarding makes them productive.

The typical failure mode is not malice or incompetence. It is the absence of a plan.
Nobody pre-provisioned the laptop because nobody checked when it needed to be ordered.
Nobody requested repository access in advance because the team assumed IT would handle
it. The service portal request for bespoke software was never submitted because the
new joiner did not know what they needed and nobody told them. Each of these is a
serial wait, and serial waits compound.

This policy defines what must happen before day one, what must happen on day one,
and who is responsible at each step. It is not aspirational. It is a checklist with
owners.

---

## Scope

This policy applies to every new joiner: permanent employees, contractors, and
consultants who need system access to do their work.

---

## Pre-Joining Phase (Manager's Responsibility)

Everything in this section must be complete before the new joiner arrives.

### Two weeks before start date

- [ ] Hardware ordered and delivery date confirmed. If bespoke hardware is required,
  this window is often not enough - order earlier.
- [ ] Work email account created with a known first-day password.
- [ ] Building access pass requested from facilities.
- [ ] IT notified of role, required software tier, and start date.
- [ ] Role access checklist submitted to IT from [ROLE-ACCESS-POLICY.md](ROLE-ACCESS-POLICY.md).
  Submit everything on the checklist at once, not item by item as discovered.
- [ ] Onboarding buddy assigned. The buddy is a peer, not the manager.
- [ ] Journey Recorder created for the new joiner from the
  [Onboarding-Journey-Recorder.md](../Templates/Onboarding-Journey-Recorder.md) template
  and shared with HR, IT, and the buddy.
- [ ] Day-1 logistics sent to the new joiner (see Day 1 Logistics in the Journey Recorder).

### One week before start date

- [ ] Hardware confirmed delivered and provisioned.
- [ ] Email account confirmed working.
- [ ] Building access confirmed.
- [ ] Mandatory induction sessions booked in calendar.
- [ ] Mandatory training links sent to new joiner's personal email.
- [ ] Buddy briefed on their responsibilities for week one.

### If any of the above is not done by one week before start date

Escalate to the department head. A new joiner whose first day is spent waiting for a
laptop is a management failure, not an IT failure.

## Day One

### Manager's responsibilities

- Meet the new joiner at reception or the agreed entry point at the agreed time.
  Do not send someone else on day one unless genuinely unavoidable.
- Walk them through the physical space: desk, kitchen, bathrooms, fire exits, quiet
  rooms, meeting rooms.
- Introduce them to the immediate team in person or on a call - not by email.
- Sit with them while they log in for the first time and confirm credentials work.
- Walk through the Journey Recorder together. Every open item in Table 2 (access)
  that was not pre-provisioned gets submitted today.

### What the new joiner should have by end of day one

- Working laptop with credentials confirmed.
- Building access.
- Work email working.
- Communication tools (Teams/Slack) installed and joined to the right channels.
- All access requests submitted (even those that will take days to resolve).
- Journey Recorder updated with submitted requests and expected resolution dates.
- Names of the people they will work with most in week one.
- Induction schedule for week one confirmed.

### What the new joiner should not be expected to do on day one

- Deliver work.
- Attend all-hands meetings or large team gatherings.
- Remember everything they are told.
- Navigate the codebase.

## Week One

### Purpose

Week one is induction, not contribution. The new joiner cannot contribute effectively
until they understand the system, the team's ways of working, and the tools.

Trying to get contribution from a new joiner in week one almost always produces
lower-quality work and higher cognitive load for the person producing it. It also
signals that the organisation values output over foundations - a message that takes
months to undo.

### Induction schedule

Week one must include at minimum:

| Induction | Owner | When |
|-----------|-------|------|
| Role-specific technical walkthrough | Manager or lead engineer | Day 1-2 |
| Codebase or system orientation | Buddy or tech lead | Day 2-3 |
| Architecture and design decisions overview | Tech lead or architect | Day 3 |
| Team working practices (standups, reviews, comms channels) | Manager | Day 1 |
| Security and compliance briefing | HR or security team | Day 2-3 |
| HR admin (benefits, expenses, policies) | HR | Day 1-2 |

All induction sessions must be booked before day one. A new joiner with no calendar
entries for week one has been abandoned.

### Buddy responsibilities in week one

- Check in at least once a day - brief and informal, not a status meeting.
- Answer questions that would be awkward to ask the manager.
- Introduce the new joiner to people outside the immediate team.
- Review the Journey Recorder at the end of the week and flag any blocked access
  items that have not resolved.

## 30-Day Checkpoint

By the end of the first 30 days:

- [ ] All access from Table 2 of the Journey Recorder is granted or has a clear
  resolution path.
- [ ] All mandatory training from Table 4 is complete or has confirmed deadlines.
- [ ] The new joiner has attended at least one code review, design session, or
  equivalent team activity.
- [ ] The manager has had at least two one-to-one conversations (not standups) with
  the new joiner.
- [ ] The Journey Recorder reflects current status - no rows left at "Requested" with
  no follow-up date.

The 30-day checkpoint is a conversation between manager and new joiner, not a form.
The Journey Recorder provides the structure; the conversation provides the substance.

---

## 60-Day Checkpoint

By the end of the first 60 days:

- [ ] The new joiner is making independent contributions without needing to be
  directed at the task level.
- [ ] The new joiner knows who to ask for what across the organisation.
- [ ] Any unresolved access or tooling issues are escalated to the department head.
- [ ] The buddy relationship formally concludes, though informal support continues.

If the new joiner is not contributing independently by 60 days, the cause is almost
always one of three things: access was never fully granted, induction was incomplete,
or expectations were not stated clearly. Investigate the cause before attributing it
to the individual.

---

## 90-Day Review

By the end of 90 days the new joiner should be a fully functional member of the team.

The 90-day review is a formal conversation covering:

- What is going well.
- What is not going well and why.
- Whether the role description matched the reality of the work.
- What the new joiner needs to progress.
- Whether the onboarding process itself could have been better.

The last point matters. Feedback on the onboarding process is how this policy improves.
If a new joiner spent significant time blocked on access, equipment, or missing
information, that is recorded and fed back to the manager and HR so the process can
be corrected before the next hire.

---

## Accountability

| Phase | Owner | Escalation |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| Pre-joining preparation | Hiring manager | Department head |
| Day-one execution | Hiring manager | Department head |
| Week-one induction | Hiring manager + buddy | Department head |
| Access resolution | IT / system owners | IT manager |
| 30-day checkpoint | Hiring manager | - |
| 60-day checkpoint | Hiring manager | Department head |
| 90-day review | Hiring manager + HR | - |

The Journey Recorder is the shared source of truth across all phases. If something
is not in the Journey Recorder, it is not being tracked, and it will be missed.
